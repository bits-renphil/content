A program manager wears many hats. The seven phases — from exploration through tech transition — are not rigidly sequential. This chapter introduces the full arc of leading a coordinated research program and why bridging academia and venture funding requires an unusual set of skills.

Exploration & Field Mapping

PMs need to understand the landscape of research, technology, and potential performers in the relevant domains. Covers strategic field mapping, practical networking, identifying bottlenecks and key risks, and a DARPA case study (MOSIS) to illustrate these concepts in action.

Refining Ideas

Refining program ideas is an iterative process of deep thinking and open conversations. Uses the Heilmeier Catechism to rigorously evaluate programs by asking key questions about objectives, approach, risks, and impacts. Balancing ambition with feasibility through cross-disciplinary thinking.

🎨

Program Design

Taking your high-level vision and field exploration results and turning them into an actionable roadmap. The goal is not to define the solution, but to define the problem(s), frame the solution approach, and structure a program that achieves your objectives while maintaining flexibility for adaptation.

Read chapter →

📣

Communicating Vision

For any program to succeed, PMs must organize and fundraise effectively. Great ideas rarely speak for themselves. Covers how to clearly communicate with stakeholders from inception to conclusion — crafting the elevator pitch, the one-pager, and the slide deck for funders, performers, and transition partners.

Read chapter →
6
⚙️

Program Engineering

Once your program idea is approved and funded, PMs must focus on securing and organizing the teams responsible for executing the R&D. Soliciting proposals, negotiating contracts, and moving from planning to action — where strategic thinking transitions into concrete decisions.

Read chapter →
7
🚧

Program Execution & Management

The phase where most of the actual R&D takes place. While many day-to-day tasks don't depend on the PM, remaining vigilant matters. As research results come back, known unknowns become known — and crucial tweaks must be made. Active management ensures effective cross-pollination between performers.

Read chapter →
8
📺

Tech Transition

PMs ensure their project transitions to the best transition partner possible — a group with the budget to cover the project and whose incentives are aligned with long-term success. Transition is hard and where many projects fail. The only way to succeed is to engage transition partners from a program's very inception.

DARPA · 2004–2007

The Grand Challenge

Prize Competitions, Field Creation, and the Challenge Model

Between 2004 and 2007, DARPA ran three consecutive prize competitions that seeded Waymo, Aurora, Nuro, and Zoox — with under $7M in prize money. The story is not about autonomous vehicles. It is about what a well-designed challenge can do that a traditional funded program cannot: shift community belief, attract outside talent, and create the conditions for a field to take itself seriously.

DARPA BTO · 2022–2026

The Triage Challenge

Designing a Grand Challenge for a Data-Scarce, Life-Critical Domain

The DARPA Triage Challenge is a three-year open competition attempting to do for mass casualty triage what the Grand Challenge did for autonomous vehicles. Designed in part by BiTS co-director JP Chretien, it illustrates how to build challenge infrastructure — including data collection programs (RITMO) before competition launch — in domains where the usual prerequisites don't yet exist.

About BiTS

The Big if True Science Accelerator is a 15-week, part-time program run by Renaissance Philanthropy that trains scientists, engineers, and entrepreneurs to design and lead high-impact coordinated research programs at organisations like DARPA, ARPA-E, ARIA, ARPA-H, and SPRIND.

About this resource

This site collects the BiTS curriculum playbook and case study library. Content is maintained in the bits-renphil/content GitHub repository. Curriculum chapters cover the full arc of program management; case studies extract design lessons for the SPRIND challenge model.

Who is this for?

Current and aspiring program managers at mission-driven research agencies and philanthropies — including DARPA, ARPA-E, ARIA, ARPA-H, SPRIND, JEDI, and Focused Research Organizations — who want practical frameworks for designing programs that work.
