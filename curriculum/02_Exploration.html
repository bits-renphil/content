Chapter 2: Exploration & Field Mapping

BLUF: In the exploration and field mapping stage, PMs need to understand the landscape of research, technology and potential performers in the relevant domains. This module provides strategic insights on field mapping, practical networking and technical exploration tips, guidance on identifying bottlenecks and key risks, and a case study from DARPA's history to illustrate these concepts in action.

⚡Practical outcomes:

  • An overview of existing R\&D efforts
  • An analysis of the rationale behind each group's focus
  • Identification of bottlenecks within the current landscape
  • Potential new applications and capabilities that could emerge from overcoming these bottlenecks
  • A list of specific users who would benefit from these new capabilities
  • A preliminary program proposal to address these bottlenecks
  • An outline of the types of performers suitable for the contract
  • Initial ideas on the long-term translation and impact of the project

The two-pager doesn’t need to be perfect at this stage, as it will be tightened in the subsequent idea refinement module. It may also be useful to keep a longer version on hand, offering deeper technical insights, caveats, and contingencies.

🌋 Quick examples of “DARPA hard” problems

Before diving into the content, it’s useful to first list out some prototypical examples of problems considered “DARPA hard.” This will give prospective PMs in DARPA-like roles a clearer understanding of the level of technical ambition their programs should be encouraged to achieve. Classic structures of “DARPA hard” technical goals are:

  • The 10X: Improving a key scientific metric or capability or reducing measures like the size or cost of a technological capability by a factor of 10 (the classic example is miniaturized GPS in the 90s)
  • The merger: Combining two distinct fields, as the Rockefeller Foundation did in the 1930s with biology and physics, various advances from entomology and robotics, bio-inspired computing or the many recent examples of AI+X
  • The multi-tool: Developing tools with broad applications such as new scientific instruments, computer chips designed specifically for life sciences use cases, scientific software that fills a common gap or even things like the CRISPR toolkit.

💡What you will learn in this process

In pursuit of these technically ambitious goals, thorough exploration and field mapping are vital. This broad term encompasses several key activities, including:

  • 🤝Networking: Building a working knowledge and network of people and organizations whose work aligns with your program’s goal. This involves understanding both the technical details of their work and the incentives and culture of various organizations.
  • 🔎Bottleneck Analysis: Identifying the limits of current practice, highlighting key bottlenecks to progress, and crafting a clear narrative of what could happen if these bottlenecks were overcome.
  • 👥User Engagement: Learning about the practical need for your program by engaging with potential users, and understanding what existing R\&D efforts do and don’t address in this area.
  • 🧠Field Knowledge: Gaining detailed insight into the progress, challenges, and trends within relevant fields.

This knowledge is not only crucial for designing a program but is also key to effectively managing it. During this stage, a PM should develop enough expertise and a strong enough network to accurately project the probability of success, timelines, and the management needs of different project groups.

Mastering the Art of Technical Exploration and Strategic Networking

Your program exploration process will be exceptionally wide-ranging. On the technical side, you'll dive into books and papers with the intensity of a PhD student, but your path as a PM will often be far less traveled than a typical thesis topic. Your focus might lie between multiple academic sub-fields or an underexplored area between academia and industry.

On the networking side, you'll reach out to researchers and industry veterans with the fervor of a startup founder. For PMs with academic backgrounds, this aspect may feel less familiar than the technical side. Remember, while some successful companies have built things their users didn’t explicitly ask for, most first talked to everyone with an opinion. It’s crucial to understand the preferences and thought processes of all relevant groups before taking action. It’s fine to challenge conventional wisdom, but you must fully understand why that wisdom exists.

Don’t wait to start networking. It’s a mistake to read thousands of pages before beginning outreach. The temptation to learn everything beforehand is understandable, but it will slow your progress. As soon as you develop a hunch about what your program might look like, a gap in the R\&D ecosystem or a potential intersection between fields, start reaching out.

Share your ideas early. Bring your hunch to the best people possible. Be clear about what excites you, what you know, and what you don’t, and ask specific questions. Start reaching out sooner rather than later—many people will schedule meetings weeks in advance, giving you plenty of time to read between the initial email and the actual meeting.

People are often pretty open. Academics, industry veterans, and potential users are often eager to share what they believe is missing in the R\&D ecosystem. Hear them out, understand what’s possible, and what’s needed, and use this knowledge to build a solid program hypothesis that can guide your efforts.

It is enough to begin with a few key contacts. Even if you only know a few people with relevant expertise, that’s enough to start. During your meetings, ask for connections to others who can answer your questions. Most people will offer at least one name, allowing you to continue your search, rinse, and repeat as you go.

Knowing when you know enough. How do you know when you’ve done enough exploration and field mapping? A good rule of thumb is when your networking starts to "close loops." In the beginning, you’ll be introduced to many new people. As you progress, you’ll notice that most new contacts are familiar. This indicates that your understanding of the field and network is reaching the level necessary to move forward with more action-oriented steps of your program.

Find the best idea, not your best idea

As a PM, your goal is to identify a valuable idea and successfully execute a program around it. The idea doesn’t need to be entirely original or something that no one has ever thought of before. Academia often emphasizes the importance of coming up with something “qualitatively new,” but this mindset isn’t necessary in your project brainstorming role as a PM.

DARPA doesn’t have its own labs; it operates akin to a very hands-on funder. As a PM, you’re more of an orchestrator of R\&D efforts than a direct contributor. You should apply this same mindset to the conception of your program. You will be the idea’s most passionate advocate, whether it’s entirely yours, someone else’s, or a blend of both. What matters is not where the idea comes from, but how well you can rally support, manage resources, and drive the project to success. Your job is to curate the best ideas and combine them into a great program—one that you’ll be managing for years to come.

DARPA’s Comparative Advantages

When deciding what your program should achieve, it’s crucial to understand DARPA’s unique strengths. The most successful DARPA programs capitalize on these comparative advantages, leveraging the agency's structure and incentives in ways that other funding sources cannot. Key advantages of DARPA-style funding include:

  • PM Autonomy: As a DARPA PM, you have the freedom to pursue what you believe is best without interference from nitpicky panels. This level of autonomy is rare and enables bold decision-making.
  • Ambitious, Applied Goals: DARPA programs are distinctive in their combination of ambitious goals with a clear focus on a specific end user. This dual focus is uncommon in the broader U.S. scientific funding ecosystem.
  • Ability To Fill Cracks in the R\&D Ecosystem: DARPA is open to betting on areas that other funding sources avoid due to structural constraints. This allows for innovative programs that other agencies might overlook.
  • Systems Engineering-style Goals: A good DARPA program is usually (though not always) more than the sum of its parts. There are various ways of structuring a program to achieve this (e.g. the collaboration vs. competition tradeoff) but perhaps the most important technique is the use of teams that are specifically focussed on the challenges of integrating project outputs and/or designing and executing (sometimes quite complex) evaluations that clarify and drive performers toward the program goals.

Make the most of DARPA’s comparative advantages by designing programs that no other funding body would be able to support. This is where DARPA truly excels.

With this overview complete, let's move on to bottleneck and gap analysis and how it can help you identify the opportunities and challenges your program will face.

🚰 Bottlenecks and Blockers

ARPA-style projects aim for more than incremental progress. DARPA is renowned for pursuing “big if true” projects with transformative potential. One very useful way of identifying high-leverage investments is to characterize bottlenecks and blockers in a domain.

  • Bottlenecks: Highlight major obstacles to progress (typically capabilities or major rate-limiters) to demonstrate the potential upside and justify the program as a high-leverage use of funds.
  • Blockers: Identify key technical challenges that are “blocking the bottleneck” and thus must be overcome (or confirmed to be impossible/impractical).

Differentiating between bottlenecks and blockers

Bottlenecks and blockers are fuzzy concepts. The following table provides definitions, examples, and heuristics to help clarify each.

Category Bottlenecks Blockers
Definitions Bottlenecks are major limitations or capabilities that, if solved or created by a program, would enable others to do something you care about. Blockers are technical challenges that must be overcome to remove these limitations or enable the capabilities.
Heuristics Bottlenecks are rate-limiters on progress. Think of them as the factory machine that slows down production. Identifying bottlenecks: Ask, "What needs to change for group A to do B?" If "C" is the answer, then C is the bottleneck. Blockers are potential scientific or technical barriers that, if confirmed to be impassible with current knowledge/tech, could halt your program. Identifying blockers: Ask, “What assumptions am I making that, if incorrect, would undermine the entire program?”
Spaceflight Example A bottleneck to frequent and cheap spaceflight is the development of reusable launch systems (a capability that significantly reduces cost). Blockers to developing reusable launch systems include whether rocket engines can consistently restart and whether control systems can be developed that can land a rocket successfully.
RNA vaccine example A bottleneck to rapidly developing vaccines was creating specific antigens on demand. A blocker for RNA vaccines was whether it was possible to develop a reliable mechanism for encapsulating the RNA.

To design effective programs, PMs must precisely identify bottlenecks and relevant technical blockers. General statements like “cost is the major bottleneck to carbon removal” are a starting point but lack the technical specificity needed. PMs need to drill down into the actionable, technology-based drivers of these issues. Your broad technical expertise, often requiring more than just knowledge in your own research area, is critical for this task.

Strategies for Addressing Bottlenecks and Blockers

1. Addressing Bottlenecks:

  • Overcome Directly: Shrink or optimize a critical system, such as reducing a device from the size of a shipping container to a briefcase.
  • Go Around: Eliminate the need for the bottleneck altogether by finding alternative approaches.

2. Tackling Blockers:

  • Prioritize: Don’t delay addressing major technical blockers, as they can make or break your program. Focus on these early, either by tackling them directly or finding workarounds. (To read more on this, check out this article from Google X’s Astro Teller’s: Tackle the monkey first)

Using written diagrams and tools can help identify bottlenecks and blockers in your space. Even if you don’t need them for your own understanding, they are valuable for communicating and selling your program. Here are some tactics for teasing out blockers and bottlenecks:

  • Diagrams:
  • Drawing block diagrams and fishbone diagrams can help identify the causal connections in a system and enable you to trace a high-level bottleneck to an actionable source.
  • Sankey diagrams can help identify what the biggest contributors to some flow are — for example, identifying why something is expensive.
  • Ballpark Estimates
  • Ballpark Fermi estimates and breaking down the physics behind a system help identify bottlenecks and blockers. Sometimes this is too crude a tool. But sometimes it can lead you to insights that rule out certain approaches. For example, if scaling a technology requires an unrealistic amount of a rare material, that’s a red flag.
  • Engaging Skeptics
  • Ask Experts to Elaborate: When experts doubt an idea, dig into their reasoning. These doubts often highlight key blockers. If a blocker proves insurmountable, it might be wise to reconsider the program.
  • Finding Key Constraints
  • Talk to Potential Users: Understand user requirements that could be make-or-break for your program. For instance, if a product needs to be carried on someone’s back for 100 miles or be under $100, these constraints inform the bottlenecks and redound to key technical blockers.
  • Drill Down
  • Once you’ve identified a bottleneck and its technical blockers (let’s say cost is the bottleneck to carbon removal through enhanced weathering and the blockers include availability of finely crushed rock and sufficient measurement and verification systems), ask if the blockers are bottlenecks (capabilities or limiters) and see if you can figure out the key technical blockers to these things. Then keep going!

📚DARPA’s MOSIS: A Case Study in Field Exploration

The MOSIS program from the 1980s is a classic example of how great programs can often emerge not from strokes of genius, but from straightforward field exploration and decisive action. This case study will highlight the early stages of the project and consider how different decisions could have led to alternative outcomes.

The R\&D Landscape and Bottlenecks

In the late 1970s, printing a single chip wafer could cost researchers the equivalent of tens of thousands of dollars today. This was especially problematic since many applications required custom-designed chips. Securing a chip run on a manufacturer’s fab was also time-consuming and caused researchers major headaches. Manufacturers who used their excess fab capacity to print bespoke chips tended to use their own specialized syntax for encoding designs. Getting designs to conform to each manufacturer’s standard was a major tax on researcher time. These cost and logistical challenges were major bottlenecks, hindering academic progress in a rapidly growing field. Additionally, if a research project led to commercialization, researchers had to worry about incumbent competitors having access to their chip designs—a significant concern that added to the inefficiency of the system.

Mead and Conway’s Contributions

A Caltech researcher and a Xerox PARC manager joined forces to come up with a better solution. The first of these characters was Carver Mead. Mead was a Caltech EE professor who had also co-authored a 1976 RAND report emphasizing that the integrated circuit revolution had many orders of magnitude of improvements ahead of it. He felt that for the field to achieve this theoretically possible progress, the research ecosystem needed much better scaffolding for ambitious design projects. To support this vision, he began developing a course at Caltech where students could design and manufacture their own chips, but he faced several obstacles: the lack of industry standards and the high cost of chip runs.

Mead tackled these issues by developing a general set of protocols from the disparate standards in industry. This would allow students to learn the applied skills they needed while ensuring that their expertise could be broadly applicable. Mead also knew that chip runs would be prohibitively expensive, so he worked on ways to pack multiple chip designs onto a single wafer efficiently. The difficulty wasn’t just in packing the chips geometrically, but also in ensuring that the designs could be easily communicated to manufacturers and worked well on their machines.

Realizing the magnitude of the task, Mead partnered with Lynn Conway, who was Head of LSI System Design at Xerox PARC. Conway brought her own expertise to the project, and together, they developed the VLSI design course, where students designed, tested, and improved their own chips. Conway’s Xerox PARC was the first manufacturer to collaborate on the course, and their efforts culminated in the successful pilot of the course at Caltech.

The curriculum and methods they developed were codified in the textbook Introduction to VLSI Systems, often referred to as the “Bible of VLSI design.” Conway soon taught a second version of the course at MIT using this textbook, and the course began to spread. In the Spring of 1980 course batch, less than three years after the course’s inception, 171 projects from 15 universities were submitted to HP for fabrication. Mead and Conway’s work demonstrated that some version of fabless design was now possible for academic computing researchers. They were succeeding in their goal to "totally isolate the designer from all the trivia that fabrication requires."

DARPA’s Involvement

It was around this point that DARPA PM Danny Cohen began to take over the program from Mead and Conway. Cohen’s goal was to scale the program to include: more manufacturers, more runs, seamless transmission of designs via the ARPAnet, reduced error rates, faster runs, increased cost savings, and an expansion of service to essentially all university research groups and government computing contractors. Under Cohen’s leadership, the program evolved into what became known as MOSIS. A more complete description of what DARPA did with the program from there is available in the linked MOSIS write-up. I’m pausing the story here, though, because the early years of what would become MOSIS provide much to think about that is instructive for PMs beginning to understand exploration and field mapping.

Lessons from MOSIS

MOSIS exemplifies how thorough exploration and field mapping can lead to impactful programs. DARPA picked up the project after the following key milestones had been achieved:

  1. There was a strong belief the integrated circuit revolution was just getting going. Many researchers felt something should be done to facilitate more ambitious design research.
  2. There were well-known cost, time, and IP inefficiencies in the current system that limited researchers’ ability to conduct experiments and commercialize their best ideas. These were clear bottlenecks.
  3. Mead had developed a loose set of general VLSI design standards.
  4. Mead and Conway had begun to collaborate on the course.
  5. They successfully piloted the course at Caltech.
  6. They wrote a textbook that enabled the course to expand to other schools.
  7. The course then spread to many more schools and utilized more manufacturers.

Mead and Conway had essentially built an MVP, begun to scale it slowly, and needed someone to do what they were already doing, but more reliably and more ambitiously. Of course, you will most likely not be so lucky as to stumble onto something like Mead and Conway’s prototype course waiting for your efforts. However, the fact that Mead and Conway were so proactive and completed so many steps before turning the program over to DARPA makes this a very instructive example.

It is fantastic that Mead and Conway made those seven bullets happen on their own, but your job is to carry out your exploration in a way that enables you to find program ideas much earlier on in their development than that.

Hypothetical Scenarios

It is not hard to imagine that a PM thoroughly exploring the technical landscape and reaching out to folks in the industry could have begun working on this idea several years earlier. Consider how a PM could have intervened earlier:

  1. Finding an Industry Partner Yourself: A PM might find someone like Mead who has an idea but needs funding and industry connections to get started. The PM could help find collaborators like Conway to drive the project forward. This hypothetical Mead might not know a Conway or much about the industry at all. The PM might have to spearhead the industry engagement aspect and find a Conway-like figure to serve as the industry-savvy performer themself.
  2. Sourcing a Dedicated Contractor: A PM might discover an academic with an idea and an industry designer who can execute it, but you might have to find completely different performers to actually run the program. Many people have good ideas but only want to focus on their specific job, no more.
  3. Coming Up with the Idea: A PM might need to generate the idea themselves through extensive reading and networking, then find the right people to develop it. This would involve bringing the ideas to individuals like Mead and Conway to see if they are possible. Since times have changed, the PM might also need to fund a third group to broker agreements between R\&D groups and manufacturers – or to fund a group to operate a pilot-scale fab for chip types that aren’t popular with existing manufacturers.

The more vigorously you network and read the technical literature, the more likely you are to come across a ripe but undeveloped project idea. This is all-important in almost all cases. In this one specific case, it was less critical because Mead and Conway were exceptional individuals—more entrepreneurial and dedicated to improving chip design than most would be in their position. More commonly, individuals in their position might come up with the idea and then forget about it, or they’d discuss it at a conference bar, say someone should do it, and then go back to their actual jobs. If a PM does their job excellently, they will find these ideas that often float into the aether and die at conferences because it’s nobody’s job to work on them.

✏️The Little Details: Networking and Approaching Conversations

With the broad topics covered, let's close this module by diving into specific strategies for navigating networks and approaching initial conversations. Much of this is common sense, and some might not align with your style, but each point represents a solid default approach worth considering.

Strategies for Navigating a Network

Designing a program often involves navigating networks you may not be familiar with and creating new ones. This can be new territory for some PMs, especially those used to interacting with individuals within their own field. As a PM, you need to be comfortable reaching out to anyone who could be useful to your program. You might find yourself in conversations with people you wouldn’t have interacted with in your previous roles. This cross-cutting nature of networking is one of the joys of being a PM. Some people will reject your outreach, but not as many as you might think. You may enter calls knowing less about people’s work than you’re used to, but that’s okay. In projects like these, nobody is an expert on everything—that’s part of the job.

Given the wide-ranging nature of your outreach, setting clear goals for your networking will help you prioritize. Here are some goals to consider:

  • Finding potential collaborators – Potential staff, performers, or others who could help.
  • Identifying potential “customers” and transition partners – Organizations or individuals who might benefit from your program and be willing to pay for it or otherwise support it, at some point.
  • Unearthing unexpected directions – Discovering new technical and strategic possibilities you hadn’t considered.
  • Understanding community perspectives – Learning what different groups think about your program and its approach.
  • Identifying bottlenecks and blockers – Spotting challenges and obstacles early.
  • Understanding technical risks – Gaining insights into potential issues with your program’s direction.
  • Learning from analogous efforts – Talking to people who’ve tried something similar before. This can provide exceptional operational lessons for a PM.

Depending on your goals, tailor your outreach accordingly:

  • For potential collaborators, aim to speak with decision-makers, such as PIs or equivalent authorities in companies. While it may be easier to reach postdocs or staff researchers, decision-makers are the ones who can commit to your project.
  • For potential “customers,” connect with decision-makers who can influence the adoption of your technology. However, if you need to understand how technology might be used, talking to the end-users can be incredibly valuable—even if they don’t have purchasing authority within their org.
  • To understand bottlenecks and blockers, talk to a wide range of experienced individuals. Bottlenecks and blockers often become apparent only with enough context and experience, so gather multiple perspectives to avoid biased views.

Whether or not you’re part of a network affects how you approach it:

  • When you’re part of a network: Start with the people you have the most social capital with—those you can ask “dumb” questions and who are likely to be most helpful.
  • When you’re not part of a network: Before diving in, read some literature (blog posts, papers, books) to get a basic understanding. These resources might not be perfect, but they’ll give you a starting point for people and questions. Don’t spend too long on this; the goal is to start conversations. Once you have some initial questions, reach out to corresponding authors or relevant firms and go from there. Attending a conference or visiting a department can also be a great way to meet people.

It can be tempting to aim for the highest-status individuals in a network, but that’s not always the best strategy. Focus on the people most useful for building what you’re trying to build, not just those with the most social capital. The “best” people can sometimes prove most helpful to you, but don’t be surprised if someone with a less famous name is far and away the most useful person to you. The existing R\&D ecosystem often rewards behaviors and work that are unrelated to what makes DARPA-style projects succeed.

At first, many conversations won’t seem useful, and that’s okay! Your goal is to emerge from your exploration with a single great idea that, if executed well, could make a big difference. It’s crucial to develop a solid understanding of and good relationships with the groups relevant to your program. You’ll quickly learn which people and resources are the most useful to take the time to know and understand. But this will be a learning-by-doing process. You’ll learn how to avoid useless connections as you go.

A common pitfall is over-indexing on what academic researchers think is important and possible. Academics write the vast majority of papers, making them good starting points, and they’re generally easier to contact. However, academic research priorities often differ from those needed to have a large impact unless your goal is explicitly to advance an academic field.

Tactics and Tools for Building Your Network

  • LinkedIn: Use LinkedIn’s 2nd-degree connection feature to identify mutual contacts who might introduce you to someone you want to meet.
  • Twitter: If there’s a Twitter research influencer in a relevant area, DM them with a short blurb about who you’re trying to connect with and your contact information.
  • Reach Out Early and Often: Don’t hesitate to reach out, and always follow up. Many people are just busy, not uninterested. A polite follow-up can often get you a response.
  • Institutional Affiliations: Pay attention to where people have worked, and when, to identify potential connections or insights they might offer.
  • Coauthors: Look at coauthors on papers for additional connections.
  • Keep a Spreadsheet: Track the people you’ve contacted, their responses, and any relevant details. This will help you manage the many conversations you’re likely to have.
  • Non-Academic Contacts: It’s generally harder to reach people in non-academic organizations, but it’s crucial. Here are some strategies:
  • Former Employees: They often know current employees and can provide valuable insights.
  • Failed Startups: Talking to people from failed startups in the same space can be more productive than contacting successful ones.
  • Cold Emails: Organizations often have standard email formats—figure these out to send cold emails directly.
  • LinkedIn Messages: Although it has a low response rate, it’s still worth trying.

Strategies for Approaching Conversations

To ensure your conversations are productive, consider these strategies:

  • Do Your Research: Before the call, read their CVs, skim their papers, and visit their website. This lets you ask useful questions and helps ensure they like talking to you. People who enjoy the conversation are more likely to help you.
  • Have a Concrete Question: Go into the call with at least one specific question you want answered. This keeps the conversation focused and gives the person a clear understanding of what you’re trying to learn. People are more likely to talk to you if you have clear goals.
  • Ask for Recommendations: Always ask, “Who else should I talk to?” and if they recommend someone, ask, “Can you make an introduction?”
  • Follow Up: After the conversation, send a thank you note and remind them of any introductions they offered to make.

Most of this is common sense, but doing the little things right can make a big difference in how smoothly this process goes!

There are many tools to choose from, including Otter, Read.ai or Fathom. AI-powered synthesis tools can then help you summarize across these transcripts and extract useful insights, consensus, points of disagreement, etc. and can even extract various types of documents that can act as useful drafts to get you started. There are a variety of tools available so try some things out. Personally, I save transcripts to a folder on my google drive and then interact with them via Google’s NotebookLM tool. Mileage may vary depending on various factors ranging from the quality of transcriptions during highly technical conversations with lots of rare words and acronyms to your comfort engaging with your AI research assistant(s), but trust me when I say these things are worth a shot. Of course, these tools won’t help for in-person conversations so I’d encourage you to write down some notes on those as quickly as possible while the details are still fresh. We knowledge-economy workers are going to have to become just a little bit cyborg if we want to achieve the efficiency gains necessary to succeed!
