# 1️⃣ BiTS Overview

# **The Big if True Science (BiTS) Accelerator**

There are countless ambitious research programs with high potential for social impact that are not a good fit for academia, but also need substantial development before they’re suitable for corporate or venture funding (or are simply non-commercial social goods). Examples include everything from the development of mRNA vaccines to the foundations of the internet. Overcoming this Valley of Death requires ambitious and patient funding from governments and philanthropies. 

Fortunately, there has been a recent surge of new philanthropic and public funding sources for ambitious, not-for-profit research programs. ARPA-H alone has a $3 billion budget to support breakthrough biomedical ideas and ARIA, the new UK ARPA, has about $1B. There’s also JEDI and SPRIND in Europe, nascent agencies or proposals in Japan and South Korea, and of course the “mature ARPAs” in the US (DARPA, ARPA-E, and IARPA). New non-profit models like the Focussed Research Organizations (FROs) launched by Convergent Research have a similar scope and shape. These organizations are positioned to play an essential role in supporting the next generation of high-impact technologies, but whether they succeed or fail is largely dependent on talent. 

Leading mission-driven, translational, and finite coordinated research programs like the ones ARPA’s and other new science funders hope to establish requires an unusual set of skills, including field exploration and mapping, designing programs for impact and transition, navigating diverse institutional incentives, calibrating and communicating mission and vision, and defining and measuring quantitative metrics to track success across a complex program with multiple integrated technical areas. 

**This playbook attempts to collect some skills, lessons learned, use-cases, and stories that we’ve found helpful over the years. It's not a comprehensive guide, and every agency, program, and program manager is different, but we think it's a good place to start.** 

**🎩 A Program Manager Wears Many Hats**

The leader of a coordinated research program needs to wear many hats. This is one of the reasons why the job is so hard! These hats roughly correspond to the different phases of a program, from ideation to transition. Each phase is almost akin to a completely different job and a great PM should be able to wear them all (often at the same time). 

1. [Exploration (sometimes called Field Strategy)](#bookmark=kix.wfmp3y8e6bap)  
2. [Refining Ideas](#bookmark=kix.68thdet46b3b)  
3. [Program Design](#bookmark=kix.sfuqpns877xo)  
4. [Communicating Vision](#bookmark=kix.ow47fw6zmzbh)  
5. [Program Engineering](#bookmark=kix.edkvbbcgq5fx)  
6. [Program Execution & Management](#bookmark=kix.is4mq0u6xr9j)  
7. [Tech Transition](#bookmark=kix.powhvgqgwcz7)

It’s important to note that these phases are not rigidly sequential. They bleed into each other and you need to work on several at the same time: it’s important to consider tech transition even while doing exploration and field mapping — keeping your eyes out for who will shepherd the technology beyond your program and what those people care about; you will need to update both your program design in real time as you execute on it and run into previously unknown unknowns; you should have a communicable vision from day one and update it as you explore and refine ideas.

## **🗺️ 2\. Exploration and Field Mapping**

[Chapter 2 Audio Version](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1vmfFViZkJuHRdytSXwxNJUXfbRhpE6w3/view?usp=drive_link)

🏆 The goals of exploration and field mapping are to:

1. Have a working knowledge and network of the people and organizations working in and adjacent to your program’s goal  
2. Understand current practice well enough that you can evaluate people and ideas: who is good; who is moving slowly because they are slacking and who is merely working on something incredibly hard; whether you are asking performers to do something impossible or merely very hard.  
3. Understand the limitations of current practice well enough that you can go several layers deep and identify unintuitive gaps or bottlenecks.   
4. Begin to get a picture of the “social dynamics” of the domain: who is really excited by hard problems and collaboration and who just wants funding to do what they’re already doing in the labs?   
5. As your conversations and ideas evolve, it's also valuable to socialize your ideas (or parts of them) early on and get feedback and/or buy-in from key members of the community. 

| 💡 Your role here is similar to a startup founder doing customer exploration. |
| :---: |

You probably come into a program with a hunch. If you don’t; create one. Exploration without an initial hunch to at least bias your search is far less effective. The point of a coordinated research program is to reach a goal. It’s tempting (and a commendable urge) to leave yourself completely open to serendipitous goals. It’s counterintuitive but having a goal makes it more likely to find different goals because you have something to compare to and contrast against.  

Field mapping is the process of figuring out the state of a “field” — the rough interconnected ideas. Who is working on what? What things are not being worked on? Why?

Some concrete tactics for exploration and field mapping:

* Create a list of research programs that have attempted something similar in the past and talk to the people who ran them. (If you think that nothing like what you’re thinking about doing has been done, you’re almost certainly wrong.)  
* Create a list of 100 researchers and companies that seem to be working in or peripheral to your goal and talk to them.  
* Read a few review papers in order to identify people who might be good initial nodes in a network to reach out to is a reasonable starting point,  
* Write down intermediate syntheses, hypotheses and conclusions from conversations in long-form text. Long-form writing (as opposed to bullet points) is important because it enables you to create causal connections between different things that you’re exploring and to notice unintuitive gaps or consistent blockers, both of which can be fruitful things for a program to go after. Update these notes as you learn more. Linked notes tools like Bear or Obsidian can be useful for this, but Google Docs, paper, or the built-in notes tools in Windows or Mac are fine — whatever works best for your brain.

All of these require navigating a network. It’s tempting to do an exhaustive search of all potential people and ideas in a space, but this is a bad idea. This phase of the program should be as short as possible, but no shorter! A good heuristic for when you’ve done enough exploration and field mapping is when your network exploration starts to close loops. That is, you start being routed towards people/papers/organizations by conversations in seemingly very disparate areas.

## **💭 3\. Refining Ideas**

[Chapter 3 Audio Version](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1kfgZrYJVnJp_5MO5NLde43t1wbjfQ5Bq/view?usp=drive_link)  
🏆The goal of refining ideas is to identify:

* A concrete program goal that is both incredibly aggressive and potentially achievable within the program’s lifetime.  
* Milestones that would suggest the program is moving towards that goal.  
* The different thrusts that a program would need to address that goal.  
* The key risks to the program (see Identifying bottlenecks and Key risks) and what evidence would suggest that you have mitigated them.

In particular, you want to address the “Big Four” questions:

1. What are you trying to do? Articulate your objectives using absolutely no jargon.  
2. How is it done today, and what are the limitations of current practice?  
3. What is new in your approach, and why do you think it will succeed?  
4. Who cares? If you are successful, what difference will it make?

Refining ideas requires both thinking and talking. You need deep thinking to connect and distill the pieces of information that you’ve gathered while exploring and field mapping, and then you need to talk to people to test those ideas against the world.

| 💡 Your role here is an analyst and researcher.  |
| :---: |

Refining ideas should happen in a loop with exploration: some conversations generate an idea for an impactful sub-goal and then running that idea by those same experts (or different ones) can give you more unintuitive nuance on it. Remember, people default to talking about higher levels of abstraction so it’s up to you to make the ideas more precise. 

### **Concrete tactics:**

* Write down and iterate on a high-level Assumptions - Hypotheses - Impacts outline. That is,   
  * Write down all your assumptions about a given program concept, problem, field, etc. This should also include your problem/bottleneck statements.  
  * Write down all the things you believe are, will be, or could be true. Some of these will be predictions and others, critically, will be conditioned on things you can control, usually by funding and coordinating research.    
  * Write down some impact statements. At a high level, how do you expect the world to be different if your hypotheses hold? Be as concrete as possible. How will specific (hopefully important) things be done better, faster, stronger?  
* Use a wireframe of the Heilmeier Catechism (see below). Wireframes are useful because they force you to be very clear and succinct.   
* Circling back with people you talked to during exploration with a concrete hypothesis for them to react to  
* You can also run workshops or do Requests for Information (RFIs) if you think you need information from a broad audience, but these methods involve higher levels of noise than targeted conversations.

## **🎨 4\. Program Design**

[Chapter 4 Audio Version](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1oqu7W5x8HVqAV7wtux9GtAxhe_YV-jhW/view?usp=drive_link)  
The program design stage focuses on transforming your high-level vision and field exploration results into an actionable roadmap. The goal is not to define the solution, but rather to define the problem(s), frame the solution approach, and structure a program that achieves your objectives while maintaining flexibility for adaptation. By the end of the program design phase, you should have:

* A complete Heilmeier document with ROM budget, timeline, evaluation plan, etc., iteratively refined with colleagues and direct managers  
* A full pitch deck ready for program approval meetings, road-tested with colleagues and managers  
* Specific examples of key potential performers and methods for each technical area  
* A draft transition strategy/options at a high level  
* A well-tested argument for why this program won't happen without your intervention

| 💡  Your role here is as visionary and strategist.  |
| :---: |

### **Scientific and Strategic Balance**

Program design success requires both strategic skills and scientific intuition:

* Scientific aspects include:  
  * Assessing technical task feasibility, timelines, and milestones  
  * Identifying capable performers  
  * Evaluating whether project elements can integrate cohesively  
* Strategic aspects include:  
  * Understanding and leveraging incentive structures  
  * Clarifying roles and responsibilities  
  * Developing program structures that will enable effective collaboration and adaptation  
  * Anticipating and addressing potential challenges

While scientific challenges can be unpredictable, strategy is often within your control. Thorough program design helps prevent issues from derailing your program.

### **Key Design Considerations**

During the design phase, you'll need to address the "Other Four" Heilmeier Questions (HQs) plus four additional questions:

1. What are the risks? (Including technical blockers, transition challenges, program structure risks)  
2. How much will it cost? (Generally between $10M-$100M)  
3. How long will it take? (Consider sequencing, phase structure, milestones)  
4. What are the midterm and final "exams" for success? (Focus on designing evaluations that drive desired outcomes)

## **📣 5\. Communications**

[Chapter 5 Audio Version](https://drive.google.com/file/d/19ajUAov0o7vlV02sFK2j7xndUuayLmC8/view?usp=drive_link)

The only way your program will be successful is if you are able to convince and align several different groups:

* Great collaborators (performers, employees, etc.)  
* The people holding the purse strings  
* Whoever is going to carry the technology on beyond your program

| 💡  Your role here is to be a keynotes speaker, a general rallying your troops. |
| :---: |

In order to get any groups on board with the program’s vision, you need to have a clear idea of what it is. (This goes back to creating concrete goals).

Communicating the program’s vision to collaborators is critical because

*  People who feel like they’re working towards a shared vision do better work.  
* In part your program will succeed because people will continue to work towards its visions even after the program ends.  
* Even collaborators who are paying lip service to a vision because you can throw around serious chunks of money need to understand it to be effective. There are so many small decisions that can’t be captured by metrics that they will fail to do good work (and you might have to pull their funding) if they don’t know where that work is targeted.

### **Concrete tactics:**

* Always have an up-to-date…  
  * One or two sentence blurb or “elevator pitch” that you (or people you’re engaging with) can use for intros and high-level explainers.  
  * A one-to-two-page document that explains the current working ideas of your program.  
  * A deck of slides that you can go to in order to illustrate specific ideas in your program that are easier to communicate visually.  
* After every conversation you have, write down:  
  * Questions or points of confusion that came up  
  * Which specific things you said that resonated or fell flat  
  * Points of regret or things you could have explained better  
* Pay attention to the Pyramid Principle — start with the bottom line of the program up front “we’re working on tools to 10x the production of X” and then go into details.  
* Simplify your language as much as possible, but no further. Even technical people secretly like straightforward explanations and you will need to communicate across a number of disciplines. AI tools like LLMs can help with this.  
* Start with concrete examples and stories and then work your way to abstractions.

## **⚙️ 6\. Program Engineering**

[Chapter 6 Audio Version](https://drive.google.com/file/d/12TSZ3sG9MwTj4zW3os7h-ajycpfLbdAk/view?usp=sharing)  
Once you have a precise program design and your program is funded, you need to get into the weeds of who is doing what. This means soliciting proposals, negotiating contracts, and generally putting rubber to the road.

| 💡  Your role here is a tactician, a salesperson, and a contract lawyer. |
| :---: |

Ultimately, no plan survives first contact with reality intact. You will have to constantly adjust your program design based on who you can get on board and what their demands are once you start getting into specifics.

In this phase, it really pays to understand the nitty-gritties of the processes in your specific organization: what kind of contracts you can write, what the process looks like, and who is in charge of it. Doing this will allow you to have as much freedom and power as you can without getting sued, fired, or going to prison.

It’s hard to give generic, concrete advice at this stage, but some meta-concrete advice:

* Do the legwork to understand the specific constraints in your organization on what sort of contracts you can write.  
* Always keep planning for transition in mind, especially around IP.  
* Make friends with your legal team!  
* Read the fine print.  
* Everything is negotiable. Many organizations may say “this is how we do things. Full stop” but given the right incentives, that is never the case.  
* Negotiating contracts always takes longer than you expect.  
* Keep in mind that nothing is a done deal until the legal contract is signed and the money is out the door. (And even then, things can go south.)

## **🚧 7\. Program Execution & Management**

[Chapter 7 Audio Version](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1hiegApr01UVSlsVBqSgKFU5ARFiunA3_/view?usp=sharing)  
Program execution is when the “actual” research happens: contracts are signed, money is out the door, and researchers are measuring, mixing, tinkering. Ironically, as the program leader there will not be as many things for you to do on a daily basis.

| 💡  Your role here is as a project manager and CEO. |
| :---: |

Treating projects within the program as “fire and forget” is often a mistake for several reasons.

1. Work expands to fill the time allotted to it. Without tight feedback loops with people doing the hands-on work, whether they are external collaborators or employees, things will often take longer in a compounding way. This isn’t to say you should be breathing down their necks, but it’s important to touch base regularly.  
2. Success will often require making adjustments to the program over time. The more in the loop you are, the smaller those adjustments need to be to keep things from going off the rails. There is a tension here because you also don’t want to micromanage.  
3. Some of the power of a coordinated research program comes from cross-pollination between the different projects. It takes work to make sure that they are talking to each other regularly.

(Of course, the relative importance of these different factors depend on what sort of program you have and what kind of program leader you are.)

### **Some concrete tactics:**

* Have regular in-person check-ins with performers. It’s one thing for people to say “everything is fine” on the phone but a very different thing when you can see the lab.  
* Have gatherings for any external collaborators with plenty of unstructured time.  
* Be willing to kill projects. It’s incredibly hard, but can free up resources for other work.  
* Be willing to double down on projects that are going very well.

## **📺 8\. Tech Transition**

[Chapter 8 Audio Version](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1KowHjYOFGTBcUq7P3wP3a468Tk290kDN/view?usp=sharing)  
Program managers must think about tech transition when they approach every single strategy decision from program conception and onward. Phrases like “transition” or “translation” are often appended to the back half of images describing the R&D pipeline as something of an afterthought to the basic research stages. That is even how many in academia and industry think about this, but that is not how program managers should approach the problem. Successful programs need people to fund their work beyond the initial, temporary payer – such as DARPA.  “Overcoming technical risks in critical technology systems” is not the whole job of a project. Projects must accomplish this goal in such a way that if need be, other payers are eager to fund the work until the job is done. 

Program managers should have the ambition to pursue projects that industry deems too risky, but they need to pursue their work in such a way that at the end of the programs initial funding period – often around five years – someone is eager to pay to continue the mission. Who these payers are can take many forms: venture capitalists, the armed services, follow-on NSF or DOE applied research grants, private sector customers, mission-driven philanthropies, the list goes on. And while the list can seem almost endless and impressively diverse, each category of funder has different needs and goals. Each tends to have its own, separate set of preferences for what makes a good investment. While it will be tough for program managers to convince any given follow-on funder to loosen their constraints or change their preferences, each group tends to be quite open to communicating their constraints beforehand and providing clear hurdles that, if overcome, would make the program an attractive investment.

This communication must happen concurrently with project conception. It is perfectly acceptable to decide that you do not wish to build for a specific set of users. It is even acceptable to decide after a conversation that your program clearly solves a clear user need even if the user does not understand how they will incorporate your completed project yet. There are cases like this from DARPA history. One prominent example is [DARPA’s Autonomous Land Vehicle program](https://www.freaktakes.com/p/the-autonomous-land-vehicle-pilots) which helped usher in much of DARPA’s [groundbreaking autonomous driving vehicles](https://www.freaktakes.com/p/an-interview-with-chuck-thorpe-on) at CMU. This broad area of work was pursued in spite of the Army not expressing a strong desire for driverless military machines. 

While DARPA did pursue the project in spite of only modest interest from the potential user, countless conversations were had with the army before the project was ever funded to understand their needs and potential use cases. This is key. Fluid communication with potential follow-on funders will enable program managers to ensure they are “technically de-risking” the right areas. 

As a crude example of how to think about this, let’s consider the different perspectives of academics and venture capitalists. The preferences of academics vary, but in general the profession rewards things like novelty, being the first to a problem area, and filling the largest “holes” in the literature. Academics often have a rough priority queue in which they rank order the “importance” of problems accordingly. When selecting projects, academics select problems that fall at the top of their particular priority queue. Venture capitalists have a rather different priority queue. They think of projects as having two large buckets of risk: technical risk and business risk. Additionally, they often prefer to invest in projects that have one kind of risk, not both. Disregarding business risk for a moment, let’s just examine the two groups’ different perspectives on what types of technical risks are worth working on. While academics tend toward the problem that fills the biggest hole in the literature, venture capitalists tend toward wanting the problem solved that is the biggest barrier to enterprise success — even if it is not not “qualitatively new” or considered groundbreaking. With this being the case, the rank orders of the two lists of “technical risks worth attacking” might be only modestly correlated, if at all. 

It is the program managers job to understand both groups and build their program accordingly. The venture capitalists might sometimes ask for less ambitious technology that is more sure to work. The PM should understand their constraints well enough to know what kinds of projects VCs would invest in, but is not obligated to take every piece of their advice. After all, it is the PMs who are paid to come up with ambitious programs, not follow-on funders. The goal of interacting with the follow-on funders early on is to answer the question, “What would they surely fund if our performers could solve X or Y problems.” 

Failure to transition is how many programs fail to have impact, even if they hit their technical goals. Design decisions early in a technology’s lifetime have profound effects on its ability to be something that somebody wants and survive beyond the R&D cocoon. Small differences, like the ability for a new material to be created using existing equipment or interface with standard connection points, can have a huge impact on its ability to transition. Great PMs do whatever they can to plan ahead and get ahead of these issues. Academics often don’t begin investigations with things like manufacturing techniques or material cost constraints at scale in mind. While academic journals do not care about that, it is your job to obsess over it.

The following list contains an example set of concrete tactics you can use to coordinate key aspects of technology transition in the early stages of building a program.

### **Additional Concrete Tactics**

*Situations vary, these are only meant to give you an idea*

* If you are looking to do a private sector transition, find a business-minded CEO and get them to start working with a team for a year before the program ends.   
* Help a team spin up a demonstration manufacturing line or pilot plant. The Valley of Death can be much shallower if lab-stage experimentation is complemented with pilot plant tests sooner rather than later. Edwin Cohn’s WWII blood lab is just one example of this strategy streamlining the translational process.  
* Coach people building the technology on how to start thinking like entrepreneurs. Stanford Med’s [SPARK program](https://www.freaktakes.com/p/unpacking-the-idea-of-entrepreneurship) exemplifies the upside of this strategy.  
* Have a large company as a performer whose funding is contingent on spinning up a new product line around the program’s technology.  
* Make sure that the IP created by the program doesn’t become locked up in a university tech transfer office or large company.  
* Get teams to start talking to potential follow-on funders long before the program ends.  
* In projects where groups are primarily incentivized to improve on component technologies, put out a contract for specific “systems integrators” to build the improved components onto a useful system.
